Fragmentation in global financial markets: good or bad for financial stability?

Asummary of Working Paper 815 published by the Bank for International Settlements and written by Stijn Claessens in 2019.

Financial fragmentation in Europe is referred to as a “lack of full traceability of central bank reserves across borders which cannot be explained by technical or fundamental factors”, per the ECB. This means that sovereign bond yield spreads widen between EU countries. The goal of the EU is to promote free trade, promote peace and stability between member states, and have Europe work as a single market with the free exchange of goods and capital. In theory, that should mean that the yields on sovereign debt. Before the financial crisis, the spread of sovereign bonds was rather small. 10-year government bonds are almost always the benchmark security to compare spreads and the reference bond is almost always the 10-year German bund as German bunds are often seen as the safest government bond in Europe and almost always has the lowest yield. Before 2009, the spread over German 10-year government bond yields was close to 0 for all countries in the euro area, and this indicated the euro area was working as intended as an integrated capital market, but this quickly proved to be a market “misassessment” that reflected a significant underpricing of sovereign risk. During the Great Recession and the ensuing European sovereign debt crisis, spreads rose to as high as 1800 basis points, or 18% for the spread of Greek bonds over German bunds. Yields for Portuguese bonds also rose significantly. Markets in Europe were financially fragmented—money and capital were not flowing to Greece and Portugal compared to Germany despite open borders, free exchange, and the single market. In theory, the goal was for German bunds, French OATs, Italian BTPs, Greek bonds, Portuguese bonds — all euro area bonds would be seen as assets that were functionally equivalent since all countries were using the euro as their currency and the bloc worked as a single market economically. But imperfections and frictions in financial market integration led to the sovereign debt crisis and financial fragmentation.

Claessens lists 4 groups that cause fragmentation, but it is difficult to isolate individual effects and declare one as more important. First are natural barriers that prevent the free flow of capital and liquidity. Natural barriers can take the form of home bias (preferring to invest locally rather than abroad), governance and informational problems, budgeting rules that limit capital from moving across different euro area members. Second are market forces that reflect investor preferences. Third are policy interventions (excluding financial regulations). If governments implement policies that incentivize local investment or if they impose tax implications for investing abroad. Fourth are regulatory interventions such as if the Basel framework forces banks to keep higher reservers for riskier foreign investments

Sources

Claessens, S (2019), ‘Fragmentation in global financial markets: good or bad for financial stability?’, Bank for International Settlements Working Paper, No. 815, pages 1-29
