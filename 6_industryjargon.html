Defining Credit Risk Modeling Terms

Credit risk modeling is the work which models and analyzes the probability that borrowers may default on their debt. In my work as a model developer, and in my time reading job descriptions and interviewing for credit risk modeling positions, I have encountered some keywords, and in this post, I aim to demystify some of the industry jargon.

Probability of Default (PD): The description is in the name here. This is the probability that a borrower will default on their debt and the lender will lose money. Lenders don’t like losing money, so they use a series of modeling methods to try to forecast the likelihood that the borrowers they lend to will default. The most common variables used to forecast PD includes loan-to-value (LTV) ratios which is the ratio of the borrowed loan amount to the value of the collateral (if the debt is backed by a collateralized asset such as a home for a mortgage loan or a car for a car loan). Debt-to-income ratios (DTI) are also important, as are credit scores in the US. Other variables will depend on the industry and loan product.

Loss Given Default (LGD): Loss given default, or the severity of default, is the amount of the loan value that a lender is expected to lose when a borrower defaults after the lender recovers some of the defaulted money by selling the collateral. For mortgage loans, for example, the lender can try to sell the the borrower’s home to get back some (or all) of the money the borrower defaulted on.

Exposure at Default (EAD): Exposure at default is the total value of the defaulted amount, or the total value the lending institution is exposed to potentially losing if they cannot recover any assets.

Stress test: These are a series of tests that financial institutions must run to determine the ability to deal with a financial crisis. For mortgage lenders, for example, that are the expected losses if there a massive decline in home prices like during the 2007 financial crisis? During a period like this, the value of the collateral may fall below the loan amount and the borrower will be considered “underwater”. Does the financial institution have enough capital to remain solvent during difficult economic conditions?

Backtest: Backtesting is a form of model validation. Many models are forecast models in that they try to forecast how much of the money they have lended they will actually get back. This is dependent on many factors, probably most influentially, the predicted economic conditions. When backtesting, modelers will take terminated loans, go back in time, and run these terminated loans as active loans with the true economic conditions that have already happened. This allows modelers to understand how close the model tracks reality without the variability of economic forecasts.

Underwrite: This is the process by which a lender evaluates a potential borrower’s risk profile for a loan. The lender may verify the borrower’s income, assets, and debt to determine the size of the loan they are willing to lend and at what interest rate.

Basel III: Basel III is a global regulatory accord that sets the framework for risk mitigation among financial institutions. After the 2007 financial crisis, it forces banks to maintain a certain level of capital on reserve in case of default. These rules include using a Risk-Weighted Asset (RWA) approach to, which means the amount in reserve is calculated based on the risk-levels of the different assets the bank is lending for. Another rule it the Internal Ratings-Based Approach (IRBA) which allows lending institutions to use their own internal risk parameters to calculate regulatory capital.
