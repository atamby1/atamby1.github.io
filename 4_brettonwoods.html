Bretton Woods and the Gold Standard

In 1944, the major world powers got together for the Bretton Woods conference which established the US dollar as the global reserve currency based on the gold standard. The value of the US dollar was set at a fixed exchange rate of $35 per ounce of gold and any country could exchange the US dollars they held for gold at any time. The US dollar was selected because the US was seen at the time as the most stable country economically and politically and was not being torn up by WWII. The fact that the US was the reserve currency meant that international goods were traded in US dollars. When France bought TVs from Japan, they bought those TVs in USD. When Mexico bought cars from Germany, they bought those cars in USD. When the US bought anything from any other country, those goods were bought in USD. This meant that the US could never face a balance of payments crisis due to their currency being valued less than the reserve currency, because it was the reserve currency. All other countries could potentially run into this problem. If the value of the French franc fell relative to the dollar, more French francs are needed to pay for internationally traded goods, and it becomes artificially more expensive for France to pay for those goods. Valérie Giscard d’Estaing, then Minister of the Economy to President Charles de Gaulle and later President himself called this the US’s “exorbitant privilege” and found it unfair. As US gold reserves fell as France and eventually other countries swapped their USD for gold (as was their right per the Bretton Woods agreement), the gold standard was eventually replaced by the USD as a fiat currency, meaning there is no commodity backing its inherent value. Its value is rather based on the trust of the institutions issuing and using it (the US government and Treasury). The USD is still the primary reserve currency — most international trade is done in USD (gold and oil, for example, are still priced in USD); however, it no longer enjoys the preeminence it once held. As of 2022, about 60% of reserves are in USD, about 20% in euros and the remaining mostly in Japanese yen, British pound sterling, Chinese renminbi, Canadian dollar, Australian dollar, and Swiss franc.

Small aside: The Chinese people's renminbi (RMB or ¥) is the currency of China while the Chinese yuan (CNY) is the unit of currency. If you see reference to the renminbi or the yuan in news articles, know that they are the same thing and the two are basically used interchangeably. The distinction is similar to the British pound sterling (GBP or £). The British pound is the unit of account for the currency, sterling. Similarly, the Chinese yuan is the unit of account for the currency, renminbi.
