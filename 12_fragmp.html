One size may not fit all: financial fragmentation and European monetary policies

Asummary of the paper published in the Review of International Economics and written by Marie-Hélène Gagnon and Céline Gimet in 2023.

Gagnon and Gimet hypothesize that heterogeneity in the risk-taking channel in Europe could hinder European credits. This means that if markets are fragmented and capital does not flow freely across national borders to all corners of the euro area, the ECB’s single monetary policy does not successfully address all member states economic recoveries effectively.

Conventional monetary policy does not improve credit through risk-taking channels. Unconventional MPs can increase loans in peripheral countries. Monetary policy can reduce fragmentation by reducing price dispersion indicators. Unconventional monetary policy has unintended consequences like rebalancing asset mix in favor of USD-denominated assets.

From when the euro was adopted in 1999 to 2007 before the financial crisis, cross-border loans increased among euro area members, which created a credit boom in certain countries (Cyprus, Ireland Greece, Italy, Portugal, Spain), increasing systemic risk. Financial cycles became disconnected across euro area members. Implementing common requirements like the Single Supervisory Mechanism leads to asymmetries. This is because lender behaviors (capital requirements and leverage ratios) are common for all commercial banks in the euro area, but borrower-based instruments (LTV, DTIs) are left to national choices, creating heterogeneities in their implementation. These heterogeneities manifest themselves in the fact that southern Europe has higher levels of non-performing loans, insolvency, and illiquidity risks, and this limits southern European banks’ access to the interbank market.

Fragmentation generates liquidity and interest rate fluctuations between countries in the euro area that deviate from central bank targets. Theoretically, Vari (2020) shows that unconventional policy when the ECB uses LTROs (Long-term refinancing operations are a form of non-conventional policy where the ECB gave 3-year loans to commercial banks during the debt crisis) or QE to increase the supply of liquidity decreases interbank market exchanges, that is, core countries see a surplus of liquidity, but peripheral countries do not see this liquidity. Empirically, Al-Eyd and Berkmen (2013) show that conventional and unconventional policies reduced some indicators of fragmentation. Szerbowicz (2015) concludes that asset repurchase were the most efficient in reducing spreads.

The study analyzes 3 shocks: a decrease in the marginal lending facility rate, positive LTROs, and positive QE.

The monetary policy shocks increase financial integration. Interest rates and QE shocks are permanent, but limited while LTRO shocks have a positive but temporary effect. Interest rates and LTRO reduce dispersion in bank default risk across the EU and have a positive effect on the dispersion of the interquartile range of short-term interbank lending rates. Positive shock in QE and negative shock in interest rates decrease the dispersion of the long-term spread of government bonds, which is consistent with Al-Eyd and Berkmen (2013) and Szerbowicz (2015) that says that unconventional expansionary policy reduces spreads. The effect on volume of assets in foreign currency is positive after negative an interest rate shock and a positive QE shock.

Expansionary policy reduces fragmentation. Conventional policy does not increase credit volume, but unconventional LTROs and QE does. Fragmentation is still present, but expansion policy lessens its symptoms. The financial trilemma states that financial stability, national regulations, and financial integrations cannot be achieved concurrently (Schoenmaker, 2011; Obsfield, 2015). To preserve stability and improve integration, regional regulation and unified macroprudential supervision are necessary.

What I take away from this policy is that conventional policy, which targets the interest rates in the form of the marginal lending rate, reduces fragmentation in the form of price dispersion (reducing bond spreads), but does not affect credit volume. Conventional policy, in theory, reduces interbank lending rates, and that means that commercial banks have more capacity to issue loans. But fragmentation means that this easier access to loans is not felt equally everywhere. In the 2010s, companies and individuals are Germany were more likely to be approved for loans than Greek companies and individuals even though Greek banks also benefitted from the reduced lending facility rate. Unconventional policies like long-term refinancing operations and quantitative easing which focus on longer-term debt and securities do have an impact on credit volume. Indeed, the entire point of QE is to focus on volume, buying long-term debt in significant quantities to provide liquidity. QE can send money directly to non-depository institutions and provides them with the liquidity to pay off debts or invest in what they want. If the ECB buys long-term debt directly from pension funds in Greece for example provides ample volume, it provides Greece with a much needed direct injection of a high volume of liquidity, something that simply lowering interest rates cannot achieve if commercial banks still won’t buy Greek debt.

Sources

Gagnon, M, Gimet, C (2023), ‘One size may not fit all: Financial fragmentation and European monetary policies’, Review of International Economics, Vol 31, Issue 1, pages 305-340
