US Debt and Bonds

TUnited States federal government borrows a lot of money to finance its operation. By far the biggest expeneses are Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, and Defense. With interest rates now rising, an increasing percentage is now also going towards interest on accrued debt.

National debt is made up of US Treasury securities which are broken into 4 types:

  • T bills: zero-coupon bonds that mature in under 1 year. They are bought at a discount of the par value and redeemed at par. They are commonly issued with maturity dates of 4, 8, 13, 17, 26, and 52 weeks (aka 1 month, 2 months, 1 quarter, 1 trimester, half-year, year)
  • T notes: 2, 3, 5, 7, 10 years with coupon payments every 6 months. The 10Y is used as a proxy for the performance of the bond market.
  • TIPS : 5, 10, 30Y maturities. Coupon is fixed at issuance, but principal is adjusted periodically based on CPI (when CPI increases, principal is adjusted up)
  • T bonds: 20 or 30 years. 6-month coupon payments. 30Y issued quarterly.

Treasury securities are considered the safest investment as the expectation is that there is no chance that the US government will fail to pay back this debt. Other securities like private bonds issued by companies are still secure, but less so because there is a chance the company may go bankrupt. Bonds from large companies like JP Morgan or Apple are probably very safe, but people said that about Lehman Brothers and they failed during the financial crisis.

In October 2023, the bond yield for the 10Y T note rose above 5% for the 1st time since 2007. Due to high inflation in 2022 and 2023, the Fed has been increasing interest rates so now the interest rates on mortgages, car loans, private loans.. all loans are higher. Freddie Mac’s PMMS is at about 8%. Since newly issued T notes are offering higher interest rates, people want to sell the notes they currently hold which don’t return as much interest, but the high supply and low demand for low-yield notes is lowering the price of these notes from their par values, and thus increasing the yields.

Yields are the coupon rate divided by bond price. If the FRBNY issues a $100 bond with a 10% coupon rate, the bondholder gets $10. But if new bonds now are getting 12%, that bondholder could now be getting $12. So the bondholder wants to sell his 10% bond so he can buy the 12% bond, but no one wants his 10% because they could also just buy a new 12% bond. So he sells it for less than $100. He probably sells it for $83 to have a yield equivalent to the new coupon rates ($10/$83 = 12%). Price of bonds move inversely to their yields. Bond yields impact borrowing costs for all loan products. Also the falling value of bonds have a negative effect on balance sheets. If banks bought $1 million in Treasury securities at 3% interest, but new debt now returns 5% interest, the value of 3% debt the bank is already holding is now worth much less and so they all of a sudden have much less capital in their books.
